# Lore

## The World

The world of Cosmic Cults is not named — it is simply "the world," because those who live in it have different names for it depending on what they believe it to be. To the Void Seekers it is a wound in the fabric of reality. To the Flesh Weavers it is a body to be remade. To the Star Children it is a gift from beyond the stars that must be protected.

The terrain tells the story. Corruption spreads from void tiles, eating neutral ground and warping it. Sanctified tiles push back. The boundary between them is where the war is fought.

## The Three Cults

### Void Seekers

The Void Seekers believe the universe is a mistake — a brief, painful eruption of matter in an ocean of nothing. They work to accelerate the return to void. Their units move freely through corrupted ground; their avatars unmake geometry itself.

Aesthetics: deep purple, black, negative space. Their altars face away from each other.

Key units:
- **Cultist**: anonymous figures in hooded robes
- **Acolyte**: carrying void-glass orbs that absorb light
- **Priest**: levitating, face replaced by a dark aperture
- **Avatar**: a mobile rift — a hole in the visual field

### Flesh Weavers

The Flesh Weavers believe the universe is alive and suffering. Their practice is surgery — cutting away the unhealthy, graft the strong. They reshape their own bodies first, then the terrain.

Aesthetics: deep red, organic forms, asymmetry. Their structures are grown, not built.

Key units:
- **Cultist**: stitched and scarred, carrying bone implements
- **Acolyte**: with grafted extra limbs, trailing corruption
- **Priest**: a tower of merged bodies, slowly mobile
- **Avatar**: a cathedral of flesh, corrupting every tile it stands on

### Star Children

The Star Children received a visitation — something came from outside and changed them. They are not certain it was benevolent. They sanctify in its honor anyway, because the alternative is what the other cults offer.

Aesthetics: gold, white, geometric patterns from non-Euclidean geometry. Their symbols are accurate star maps.

Key units:
- **Cultist**: wearing mirrored masks that reflect distorted light
- **Acolyte**: leaving faint glowing footprints on sanctified ground
- **Priest**: surrounded by slowly orbiting objects
- **Avatar**: a geometric solid from outside normal space

## Terrain Types

| Terrain | Nature |
|---------|--------|
| Neutral | Uncontested ground. No faction influence. |
| Corrupted | Touched by the void. Void Seekers move freely; others pay extra. |
| Sanctified | Blessed by the Star Children. Movement bonus for Star Children. |
| Void | Fully consumed. Almost impassable. Corruption value approaches 1.0. |

## Game Phases and Their Lore

**Exploration**: Cults send scouts to map unknown territory before committing resources. The fog of war is the world's unknowability.

**Combat**: Cults clash where their influence meets. Combat is not merely violence — it is a contest of whose reality will persist.

**Ritual**: Priests and acolytes perform the slow work of changing what is real. Tiles are corrupted, sanctified, or cleansed during this phase.

## The Turn Structure

Three factions. Three phases per turn. The order of action rotates, giving no single cult a permanent advantage. In the Lovecraftian tradition, the game is designed so that no player can be certain of winning.

## Tone

Cosmic Cults is not horror in the jump-scare sense. It is horror in the sense of encountering something that operates by rules you do not fully understand. The game should feel like reading a field manual written by people who believe their cause is correct and their enemy is wrong, when you can see that all three are working toward the same unmaking.

The visual style reinforces this: cel-shading removes realism, creating a distance. You are not in the world. You are observing it, which is its own kind of dread.
